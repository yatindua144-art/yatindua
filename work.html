About

My grandfather crossed the border during Partition with two Oxford degrees in history and whatever he believed about the world. At my first birthday he held me up in a crowd with one arm raised. I have been trying to understand that gesture ever since.

I am a policy analyst, photographer, and writer based in Vancouver. I have done fieldwork in rural Jharkhand, attended summits in Riyadh, built an Indigenous maternal health app in Bella Bella, and written poems published in a queer South Asian magazine. I went to London on my own money and stayed in a friend's dorm for ten days. I went to Jharkhand without an assignment and came back with photographs.

The through-line in all of it is the same: I go to the places. I try to understand what I find. I come back and write it down.

Photography

They Have Known Me All Their Life

Jharkhand, India — 2023

In Jharkhand, when crops fail or a child gets sick, sometimes a woman gets the blame. She is called a dayan. A witch. Her neighbors decide. Her family often agrees. The law banned this in 2001. The law didn’t change much.

I traveled to Khunti and Murhu districts in 2023 to meet the women still living with that word. Not the violence — that was over. What I found was what comes after. Women who still fetch water. Still work the earth. Still laugh. Still sit under trees. Still answer doors.

One woman laughed when I asked about it. “They have known me all their life,” she said. “How did I suddenly become a dayan?”

I didn’t have an answer. I don’t think she expected one.
